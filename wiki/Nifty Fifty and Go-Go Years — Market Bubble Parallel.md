# Nifty Fifty and Go-Go Years — Market Bubble Parallel

**Sources:** Playing with Fire | GQG Research (Aug 2025); No One Is an Atheist In a Foxhole…or at 20x Price to Sales | CIO Perspectives (Oct 2025)
**Tags:** #bubble #nifty-fifty #valuation #Buffett #historical-parallel #concentration #go-go-years

---

## Summary

GQG's August and October 2025 papers draw explicit historical parallels between today's AI-driven market and the "Go-Go Years" of the late 1960s/early 1970s, when the "Nifty Fifty" — a basket of large-cap growth companies deemed buy-at-any-price — experienced the same narrative-driven multiple expansion before a brutal multi-year compression. The lesson: even companies with genuinely strong underlying businesses can deliver catastrophic returns for investors who pay the wrong price.

---

## Warren Buffett and the 1969 Dissolution

The anchor case study is Buffett's 1969 decision to dissolve his successful 12-year investment partnership amid the "Go-Go Years" — a period of investor overconfidence in the prospects of growth companies at stratospheric valuations. The move looked premature:

- A sharp correction led to a rebound and higher prices in 1973
- But the 1973 peak was followed by a severe crash in 1974
- Broad indexes did not recover their 1973 levels for nearly a decade (nominal, and far longer adjusted for inflation, which was significant)
- Buyers who entered in 1970–1971 — years before the peak — did not recover principal until four to eight years after the market bottom
- The crash wiped out not only optimistic investors, but major brokerage firms — including DuPont Walton, the second largest of the era

Buffett's pattern repeated before the GFC: Berkshire's cash balance built steadily as stock valuations reached new heights, enabling discounted purchases during the crisis. GQG draws the direct parallel to today.

**The Buffett Indicator (market cap / gross domestic income):**

> *"If the percentage relationship falls to the 70–80% area, buying stocks is likely to work very well for you. If the ratio approaches 200%, as it did in 1999 and part of 2000, you are playing with fire."*
> — Warren Buffett, Forbes, 2001

By mid-2025, this indicator had exceeded dotcom peak levels.

## The Nifty Fifty: Buy-at-Any-Price, Then Pay the Price

The Nifty Fifty (pharmaceuticals, imaging, cosmetics, tech) were genuinely excellent businesses — and that is the point. Even high-quality companies with real earnings growth destroyed investor returns when purchased at extreme multiples.

**The result 1973–1979:** Revenue and earnings actually grew in the low-to-mid double digits. What collapsed was the multiple:

| Company | Revenue Growth 1973–79 | Total Return 1973–79 |
|---|---|---|
| Johnson & Johnson | Strong | **-33%** |
| Procter & Gamble | Strong | **-20%** |
| Coca-Cola | Strong | **-41%** |

Nifty Fifty tech names (Xerox, Eastman Kodak, Texas Instruments, Burroughs) saw an average **80% compression in price-to-sales multiples** in the latter half of the 1970s. Most of those tech names eventually fell into reorganisation or were absorbed — the market had been prescient about their structural limits.

**The academic verdict:** Jeremy Siegel (Wharton, 1998) concluded that investors who held Nifty Fifty companies through the crash would have done "almost, but not quite, as well as the broader market" — cold comfort after a decade of underperformance.

## The Statistical Pattern: 10x Revenue Multiples

GQG reviewed every S&P 500 company that traded at ≥10x trailing-12-month revenues over the past 30 years:
- Very few maintained these multiples over time
- On average, the multiple declined **60% over the ensuing five years**
- Even the largest addressable markets eventually become saturated; competitors enter

**Today's extreme:** ~**13% of the S&P 500** (by index weight) traded at **>20x price-to-sales** as of end of September 2025 — surpassing the dotcom bubble peak of ~11%. Nvidia was at 28x P/S at that date.

> *"This is not a small-cap or micro-cap index — these are many of the largest companies by market cap on earth. To us, this is clearly a buy-and-hope."*

**Top 10 S&P 500 concentration:** At multi-decade highs by 2025, matching or exceeding the 1970s peak. The US is effectively a single-theme index.

## The AI-Specific Fragility (Oct 2025)

*No One Is an Atheist* adds current-cycle evidence to the historical framework:

- Chinese AI companies: exploded from 1,454 (2020) to **5,000+** (Sep 2025) — roughly **one new AI company every 11 hours**
- MIT study: **95% of enterprise AI projects have produced no meaningful results**
- OpenAI targets $1T in infrastructure spend and $200B in revenue by 2030 — GQG compares this to the Bank of America analyst who forecast $50T in B2B e-commerce by the early 2000s (US nominal GDP in 2025 is just below $30T)
- GPU rental prices (B200) already declining — Silicon Data rental indices showing real price compression before demand has peaked
- Vendor financing recycling: Nvidia participated in 52 VC deals in 2024 + 50 more in 2025 — direct investments equivalent to **two-thirds of annual revenue** (vs. Lucent's vendor financing at 24% of 1999–2000 revenues before it collapsed)
- OpenAI/Oracle $300B deal: for computers that don't exist, funded by money OpenAI doesn't have

**The canal analogy** (from Edward Chancellor's *Devil Take the Hindmost*): Early canals returned 50% on capital → decades of oversupply → returns fell to 5%; one in five canals couldn't pay a dividend. But canals were **profitable from day one** — unlike today's AI infrastructure projects.

**Charlie Munger quote (used by GQG):**
> *"Bull markets go to people's heads. If you're a duck on a pond, and it's rising due to a downpour, you start going up in the world. But you think it's you, not the pond."*

## GQG's Opportunity in the Ignored

Both papers end the same way: redirecting attention to real growth happening outside the AI narrative:
- **India:** Adani airports handling 20M domestic/global passengers, scaling to 90M; Adani Ports recording highest-ever cargo volumes
- **Brazil:** Digital banking payments exceeding 80% (up from 50% a decade ago)
- **European pharma:** Drug pricing uncertainty resolving; breast cancer pipeline
- **Utilities:** Indian infrastructure and US regulated utilities with structural demand

> *"Growth and innovation are not only occurring in AI-related spaces. These things are simply being ignored given the massive numbers being thrown around in the AI space, regardless of the likelihood of those figures being realized."*
