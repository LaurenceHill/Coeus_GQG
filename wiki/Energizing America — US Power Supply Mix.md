## Summary

September 2024 companion paper to [[US Power Demand Surge]] (also Sep 2024). Where the demand paper covers *why* power needs are surging (AI, EVs, fuel switching), this paper covers *how* utilities are positioned to meet that demand — and which asset compositions are best placed to benefit. GQG's view: utility attractiveness depends on (1) asset fleet composition and (2) the political orientation of their primary regulator. Not all utilities are equivalent.

## The Three Energy Sources

### 1. Nuclear

Nuclear is GQG's highest-conviction power supply thesis as of September 2024:

- US nuclear capacity: **91.5GW** across **93 reactors** in 30 states
- Generates ~**775 billion kWh**, or **~19% of total US electricity**
- US is the world's largest nuclear producer — ~30% of global nuclear electricity generation
- Scarcity asset: limited number of operators means value per plant is rising

**Key companies:**
- **Vistra Corp (NYSE: VST)** — Texas-based, nuclear + natural gas; shares up **150% year-to-date** by May 2024 on AI/data center demand expectations
- **Constellation Energy (NASDAQ: CEG)** — major nuclear fleet operator
- **Southern Company (NYSE: SO)** — nuclear + gas; favorable regulatory environment

**Behind-the-meter deals** are the emerging model:
- **Talen Energy / AWS deal:** Talen's Pennsylvania nuclear plant provides ~1,000MW to an adjacent AWS data center. AWS pays **$75–80/MWh** (10-year rate) — a **70% premium** to the $45/MWh market rate. This structure bypasses grid transmission bottlenecks entirely.

### 2. Natural Gas

Natural gas currently supplies **~43% of US electricity** (fossil fuels overall: ~60%). Key dynamics:

- Gas demand from data centers expected to drive **3.3 billion cubic feet per day of new demand by 2030**
- ~**$50B in new generation capacity** needed to meet growing demand
- Volatility profile: lower average demand, but **potentially much higher on peak days** when intermittent renewables underperform — the grid's insurance policy
- The US natural gas pipeline network: **~3 million miles**, delivering ~29.2 trillion cubic feet in 2022
- Gas utility operators saw demand tailwinds with volumes up **~40% per day from 2000 to 2022**

**Key companies:** Vistra Corp, Duke Energy (NYSE: DUK) — Carolinas, Southern Co — Georgia

### 3. Renewables

Renewables: ~**21% of total US electricity** in 2024. The IRA provided new federal funding for deployment.

- **42GW of renewable capacity** added to the US grid in 2023
- **NextEra Energy (NYSE: NEE):** largest renewable energy company; ~29GW capacity; wind = 66% of generating assets
- Solar will contribute **~60% of US electricity growth** in 2024, per EIA
- **Structural challenge:** renewables require **3x the generation capacity** of coal or natural gas due to their intermittent nature — a capital-intensive transition

## GQG's Utility Selection Framework

GQG's two-factor utility filter:

1. **Asset fleet composition** — nuclear and gas operators are better positioned than pure renewables for near-term AI demand
2. **Regulatory environment** — utilities in politically stable, business-friendly states have more pricing power and investment certainty

This framework explains GQG's preference for companies like Vistra, Constellation, Duke, and Southern — each combining favourable asset mix with supportive regulators.

## ⚠️ Tensions and Evolution

**Forward-looking contradiction:** By January 2026 (*Against the Grain*), GQG had become cautious on AI-predicated utilities — explicitly avoiding utilities whose investment thesis was premised on AI CapEx continuing at pace. Part V of Dotcom on Steroids (Apr 2026) confirms this caution: capacity auction prices skyrocketed (PJM: $30/MW-day → $329/MW-day), but this may deter new investment rather than sustain high earnings. See [[Data Center Social License Risk]] and [[Defensive Sectors — 2026 Opportunity]].

The shift is consistent: the same AI scepticism that drove GQG's tech exit also caused them to reassess utilities whose thesis depended on AI-driven demand continuity. This is flagged directly in [[US Power Demand Surge]].

**IRA Policy:** This paper assumed Biden-era IRA incentives for renewables investment — same stale-claim risk as the CCS paper. The IRA's renewable energy support faces uncertainty under Trump. Nuclear and gas are less policy-dependent.
