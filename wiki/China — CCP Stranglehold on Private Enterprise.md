## Summary

April 2023 paper — GQG's most detailed examination of why Chinese private companies are not truly "private." Despite China's remarkable economic rise (136 Fortune Global 500 companies, surpassing the US in 2020), the private sector operates under constant CCP oversight that can be weaponised at any moment. The paper traces this dynamic decade by decade from the 1980s to 2023, concluding that the risks are rising, not stabilising, as China's economic, demographic, and geopolitical profiles deteriorate. This forms the historical and structural foundation for [[China Equity Market Skepticism]] and [[SOE Reform and Corporate Governance]].

---

## The Core Paradox

Chinese private firms account for:
- **>60% of GDP**
- **~80% of employment**

Yet these "private" companies must operate under a system where the CCP has explicit legal authority to demand information, install party units inside firms, and revoke approvals without warning. Chinese companies on the Fortune Global 500 post **profit margins roughly one-third of American and European peers** — partly due to business mix (banks and utilities vs. tech and pharma), but also from the structural drag of CCP interference in decision-making.

## Decade-by-Decade History of CCP Control

### 1980s: Short-Lived Boom
Reform era began promisingly. Deng Xiaoping's reforms allowed private entrepreneurship for the first time in decades. Lenovo founded 1984; Huawei 1987. Government credibility among entrepreneurs grew — until **Tiananmen Square (June 1989)**. Post-Tiananmen leaders launched political suppression of "enemies," casting a chill over private enterprise.

### 1990s: SOE Reform, But Property Rights Never Secured
Deng's "Southern Tour" (1992) re-launched reforms. Jiang Zemin and Zhu Rongji overhauled SOEs. The "Three Represents" doctrine (2000) nominally recognised the private sector. But property rights were never secured through institutional reform — leaving a permanent opening for crackdowns. High-profile example: Jianlibao Group (one of China's largest soft drink producers) was effectively seized by local officials.

### 2000s: WTO Accession → Tighter Party Embeds
China joined the WTO in 2001. The same year, every private firm with ≥3 CCP members was required to have a party unit to "firmly implement the Party's line, principles, and policies." The **State Assets Supervision and Administration Commission** (2003) further extended state influence. During the GFC, state-run banks were given detailed guidance on which industries and companies to lend to — and at what rates. Chinese banks have traded at material discounts to global peers ever since.

### Xi Era (2012–Present): Escalating Control
Under Xi Jinping, tools have multiplied and actions have accelerated:

| Instrument | Date | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| Corporate Social Credit System | 2014 | Determines access to government procurement and credit |
| National Intelligence Law | 2017 | Requires all firms to provide data/information on national security grounds |
| CCP branch mandates | Ongoing | Firms pressured to form party branches and pledge "common prosperity" funds |
| "Golden shares" | Ongoing | Government took direct stakes in Weibo (580M users) and ByteDance (TikTok) |

**High-profile crackdowns:**
- **Wanda Group:** Hotels and entertainment assets sold in a fire sale after new regulations; founder Wang Jianlin forced to deleverage
- **Anbang Insurance:** Dismantled after $2B Waldorf Astoria purchase; chairman Wu Xiaohui sentenced to 18 years for fraud and embezzlement; company absorbed by SOEs
- **Internet platforms (Alibaba/Ant Group, Didi):** The sector that "nearly escaped" regulation — once the government decided platforms were "too important," they were brought in line overnight
- **After-school tutoring:** A $120B industry was "nearly crushed overnight" when for-profit tutoring of core subjects was banned

## The Post-COVID Charm Offensive — And Why GQG Remains Sceptical

After zero-COVID was abandoned, the government moved to woo private enterprise again:
- Vice Premier Liu He at Davos (Jan 2023): "China is coming back" and "we will keep opening up and supporting private businesses"
- Premier Li Qiang (Mar 2023): pledged equal treatment for private and state firms; property rights would be protected

GQG's response: verbal assurances are structurally insufficient when the **legal infrastructure of control** (National Intelligence Law, Corporate Social Credit, golden shares, party embeds) remains fully in place. Promises have been made in every decade and reversed when political priorities changed.
