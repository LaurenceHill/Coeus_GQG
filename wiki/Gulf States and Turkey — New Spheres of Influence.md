## Summary

May 2024 paper — GQG's case for the UAE, Saudi Arabia, and Turkey as investable emerging markets that are systematically underrepresented in global portfolios. The traditional financial hubs (New York, London, Hong Kong) are being joined by Abu Dhabi and Riyadh as economic centres of gravity. These markets are young, carry oil-dependency risks, but offer genuine early-mover advantages for investors willing to look beyond traditional Western allocations.

---

## The Structural Shift

Post-Russia/Ukraine, the global geopolitical order is accelerating a shift toward multilateralism. Key drivers:

- Western freeze of Russian national assets has prompted global capital to explore alternative safe havens
- Gulf states and Turkey are uniquely positioned as bridges between the Western order and emerging multi-polar geopolitics
- India's deepening relationship with Gulf states: UAE and Saudi Arabia are India's **3rd and 4th largest trading partners** (behind US and China)
- Qatar: one of the world's three largest LNG exporters (alongside US and Australia); provided lifeline to Europe post-Russia sanctions

## Country-by-Country Analysis

### UAE (Abu Dhabi / Dubai)

- Leading economic diversification across tourism, trade, real estate, banking, and finance
- **102 asset management firms** operating in Abu Dhabi's financial centre by end of 2023; another 125 (including hedge funds) expected to open in 2024
- Strong property rights, asset security, multicultural human capital base
- Dual-track strategy: safety/stability for global capital + genuine sector diversification

### Saudi Arabia

- Vision 2030 reform: once perceived as the world's most socially conservative major economy, undergoing remarkable social opening
- Young population: 37 million, digitally connected
- Reform trajectory: began under King Abdullah; dramatically accelerated under King Salman (2015+) and Crown Prince MBS
- Energy: leveraging oil wealth to bolster role as critical global infrastructure partner

### Turkey

- NATO member; intermediary in Russia-Ukraine conflict; controls Black Sea access (both blocking Russia's fleet expansion and securing Ukrainian grain exports)
- Simultaneous economic pragmatism: sanctions on Russia (per NATO), but also the **third-largest buyer of Russian crude** (behind China and India) — effectively a critical hub for Russian oil/gas exports to Europe and Asia
- $1T economy; real GDP growth outpacing the Euro Area dramatically:
  - Turkey: cumulative real GDP growth of ~150% from 2009 through 2028E
  - Euro Area: ~30% over the same period
- IMF-validated trajectory

## The Underweight Opportunity

GEM (Global Emerging Market) funds were significantly underweight this region as of December 2023:

| Country | MSCI EM Weight | Average GEM Fund Weight | Underweight vs Benchmark |
|---|---|---|---|
| Saudi Arabia | 4.17% | 1.63% | **-254 bps** |
| UAE | 1.26% | 0.45% | **-81 bps** |
| Qatar | 0.91% | 0.10% | **-81 bps** |
| Kuwait | 0.75% | 0.03% | **-72 bps** |
| Total Middle East | 7.09% | 2.21% | **-488 bps** |
| Total EEMEA | 12.74% | 7.10% | **-564 bps** |

The systematic underweight reflects unfamiliarity and home-country bias rather than fundamental analysis. GQG sees this as a classic early-mover mispricing.

## Risks

GQG explicitly flags:
- **Oil price dependency:** Dollar-pegged Gulf economies remain exposed to oil price cycles that can "make or break national coffers"
- **Nascent markets:** Young financial markets with limited track records
- **Political complexity:** Turkey's balancing act between NATO and Russia carries geopolitical risk; Gulf reform agendas are still works in progress
- These markets are not replacing traditional financial hubs but **broadening the opportunity set**
