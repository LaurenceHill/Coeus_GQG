# Alcohol Industry Structural Decline

**Source:** A Spirited Debate | GQG Research (May 2024)
**Tags:** #alcohol #spirits #beer #structural-change #consumer #demographics

---

## Summary

GQG argued in May 2024 that the post-pandemic hangover in the alcohol industry was not merely cyclical but concealed deeper structural damage. Falling youth drinking rates, aggressive over-pricing, GLP-1 drug adoption, concentration of consumption in heavy drinkers, and the early stages of tobacco-style regulation all pointed to a multi-decade headwind. GQG was being "very selective" in the sector, especially in developed markets.

---

## Explanation

### The Post-Pandemic Setup

Alcohol sales surged in 2021-2022 (lockdowns + consumer spending shift + price increases). 2023 brought a sharp volume correction. The industry framed this as cyclical ("hangover"). GQG disagreed, identifying structural forces:

### Force 1: Demographic Decline in Young Adult Drinking

- 18-34 year olds in the US: share who drink fell from 72% (two decades ago) to **62% in 2023** (Gallup)
- Frequency and volume also declining within those who still drink
- **52%** of people now believe even moderate drinking is unhealthy — up from 34% five years ago
- This belief is structural and likely to increase as health science advances

### Force 2: Aggressive Over-Pricing Created Structural Trade-Down

Distillers used price/mix to sustain revenues during volume declines. This backfired:
- Example: Casamigos Blanco (~$50 in New York) vs. Espolon Blanco (~$25) — both 100% agave, broadly similar quality
- Consumers who trade down may never return to the $50 bottle
- Tequila "premiumization" is reversing; sales now driven by lower price points

### Force 3: Concentrated Consumption Risk ("Right Tail Risk")

From NIH research (economist Philip J. Cook): the top 10% of US drinkers consume **74 drinks per week** and account for **more than half of all alcohol consumed**. 60% of adults consume half a drink or less per week. This extreme concentration means:
- Loss of even a fraction of heavy drinkers has disproportionate industry impact
- Cook estimated: if the top decile reduced to ninth-decile levels, total ethanol sales would fall **60%**

### Force 4: GLP-1 Drugs (Semaglutide/Tirzepatide)

Animal studies and early human evidence (published in *Nature*) show GLP-1 drugs reduce alcohol consumption. In humans taking semaglutide or tirzepatide for obesity, real-world evidence showed reduced alcohol intake. Still early innings but a structural headwind given the [[GLP-1 Impact on Consumer Industries|GLP-1]] adoption trajectory.

### Force 5: Regulatory Headwinds — The Tobacco Parallel

Ireland mandated comprehensive health labelling on all alcoholic products starting 2026 — first country in the world. Labels must state cancer risks, liver disease, calorie count, and pregnancy dangers. This mirrors early tobacco regulatory trajectory. Regulatory scrutiny was the key catalyst that finally triggered the tobacco de-rating.

---

## The Tobacco Analogy

GQG drew explicit parallel to tobacco stocks:
- Tobacco de-rated **significantly in 2017**, nine years after global volumes began declining (ex-China, from 2008)
- Investors were comfortable with price/mix sustaining revenues despite volume declines — until they weren't
- Spirits face the same dynamic, but without tobacco's pricing power advantage (alcohol is more price-elastic; trade-down options exist)

**Key insight:** The gap between structural deterioration beginning and the market recognizing it can be long — but it eventually closes violently.

---

## Beer vs Spirits

- US beer shipments in 2023 hit their lowest level since 1999
- Spirits had gained 13 percentage points of market share by volume since 2000 (each point worth ~$890M in supplier revenue)
- But that share gain engine is now stalling as premiumization reverses

---

## ⚠️ Contradiction with Later GQG View

*Against the Grain* (Jan 2026) — 20 months later — cites **Heineken** as an attractive investment at 13x earnings with mid- to high-single digit earnings growth potential. This appears to contradict the bearish stance here, but key distinctions apply:
1. This paper focused primarily on **spirits** (Diageo); Heineken is a **beer** company
2. GQG said in 2024 they would still look for "selective opportunities"
3. Heineken's valuation by 2026 reflected the de-rating already having occurred — consistent with GQG buying *after* the structural problems are priced in
4. GQG explicitly notes in 2026 that Heineken's COVID-era price increases have now been digested

**Interpretation:** Not a contradiction so much as a confirmation — GQG identified the structural decline, waited for the de-rating, then found entry. The 2026 bullish case is *price-dependent*, not a denial of the structural issues.
