## Summary

GQG's June 2023 analysis positioned CCS not as a moral issue but as an emerging investment opportunity — particularly for integrated oil companies (IOCs) with legacy infrastructure in the Permian Basin. ExxonMobil and Occidental Petroleum were identified as best-positioned, leveraging existing Enhanced Oil Recovery (EOR) assets, pipeline networks, and the dramatically improved economics created by the Inflation Reduction Act's enhanced 45Q tax credits.

---

## What Is CCS?

Carbon Capture and Sequestration involves three steps:
1. **Capture**: Removing CO₂ from point sources (industrial exhaust) or directly from the atmosphere (Direct Air Capture / DAC)
2. **Transport**: Moving CO₂ via pipeline networks
3. **Storage**: Injecting CO₂ into subsurface geological formations permanently, or recycling it via Enhanced Oil Recovery (EOR)

**History**: Technology patented in 1952; commercialised in the 1970s via EOR in the Permian Basin. US IOCs have decades of operational advantage.

---

## Why IOCs Have a Structural Advantage

- **Legacy EOR infrastructure**: ExxonMobil and Occidental operate some of the largest EOR projects globally and own the pipeline networks required for CO₂ transport
- **Scale**: Large balance sheets needed to finance sequestration hubs; government eligibility criteria (1 million ton+ capture threshold) favour large operators
- **Expertise**: Decades of subsurface engineering, injection monitoring, and reservoir management

---

## Key Technologies

### Enhanced Oil Recovery (EOR)
Injects CO₂ into oil reservoirs to extract remaining oil; the CO₂ is partially stored in the subsurface. Produces "low-carbon" fuel in a closed-loop system.

### Direct Air Capture (DAC)
Removes CO₂ directly from atmosphere using chemical sorbents or solvents. More energy-intensive and expensive than point-source capture (atmospheric CO₂ is less concentrated than industrial flue gas), but necessary for absolute emissions reduction.
- Occidental: ~$1.4B total DAC plant cost; contributed $275M for first plant; ambition of 100 DAC plants by 2035
- Occidental's Low Carbon Ventures unit expected to eventually exceed OxyChem sales (~19% of 2022 revenue)

### Sequestration Hubs
Aggregate CO₂ from multiple industrial sources → economies of scale, shared storage, reduced per-ton costs.
- ExxonMobil's Houston Hub: projected ~50 million tons per annum (mtpa) capacity by 2030; partnership includes Chevron, Dow, Linde, Valero

---

## The Scale Gap

IPCC AR6: Net zero by 2050 requires ~10 gigatons (GT) of CO₂ captured per year. Current global capacity: 44 mtpa (~0.4% of the required level). However, in 2022 alone, total commercial CCS project capacity (operational + in-development) grew to 244 mtpa — a 44% YoY increase. The gap is vast but the growth rate is accelerating.

## Policy Tailwinds

### US: 45Q Tax Credit (Pre-IRA)
- $35/ton for CO₂ used in EOR
- $50/ton for permanent CO₂ sequestration (up to 2026)
- CCUS Tax Credit Amendments Act proposed increases to $75/t (EOR) and $120/t (sequestration)
- Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act: $3.5B for Regional Direct Air Capture Hubs program

### US: Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) — Game Changer
- 45Q credits increased to **$85/MT** for industrial CCUS
- **$180/MT** for DAC (non-EOR)
- First 5 years of credits paid **upfront** — dramatically improves project economics and financibility
- GQG: "Following the IRA, the investment case is stronger than ever"

### International
- COP26 agreement to develop a global carbon market with standardised rules between governments and corporations

## Investment Framing

GQG framed CCS as transforming from a "moral quandary" to a genuine business model offering:
- Revenue from low-carbon fuels and chemicals
- CO₂-based feedstocks
- Sequestration credits (government-backed at $85-180/MT post-IRA)
- Hedge against stranded asset risk for fossil fuel producers

## ⚠️ Stale Claim — IRA Policy Risk (Post-Trump Jan 2025)

This paper was written in June 2023 under Biden-era policy assumptions. The Inflation Reduction Act's 45Q tax credits — described as making "the investment case stronger than ever" — now face material policy risk under the Trump administration (returned to office January 2025), which has signalled hostility to IRA climate provisions.

Specific stale assumptions to verify:
- $85/MT CCUS credit and $180/MT DAC credit may be reduced or repealed
- The "upfront payment for first 5 years" provision may not survive
- The $3.5B Regional DAC Hubs programme funding may be redirected or cancelled

The *structural* IOC advantage (Permian Basin legacy infrastructure, EOR expertise, hub scale) remains valid regardless of policy. But the financial case for new project development was materially dependent on IRA economics. CCS project economics should be stress-tested at lower credit values.

The CCS thesis is a long-term structural play (energy transition) that is distinct from the cyclical AI power demand discussion in [[US Power Demand Surge]], but the IRA policy backdrop underpins both theses.
