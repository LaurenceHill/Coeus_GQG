# China EV Industry — Overcapacity and Trade Tensions

**Source:** China's Electric Dream | GQG Research (Apr 2025)
**Tags:** #china #EV #overcapacity #tariffs #BYD #trade-war #subsidies

---

## Summary

China's electric vehicle industry achieved extraordinary scale through state subsidies, government mandates, and vertical integration — overtaking Japan as the world's largest car exporter and claiming ~70% of global EV registrations. But GQG's April 2025 analysis identified a structurally unstable model: overcapacity equivalent to 116% of global EV demand, financial stress from price wars, and escalating trade barriers (US tariffs reaching 154% on Chinese EVs). The solar and steel analogies suggest a familiar state-driven boom-bust pattern.

---

## The Ascent

China's automotive journey: first "Liberation" truck in 1956 → Deng Xiaoping's open-door policy (late 1970s) enabled foreign JVs → WTO accession 2001 → China surpassed the US as the world's top auto market in 2009 (and hasn't relinquished it since).

The electric pivot was policy-driven: urban pollution and congestion led Beijing to mandate EVs. By 2024, China held **~70% of global EV new registrations**. China overtook Japan as the world's leading passenger vehicle exporter.

Government support by the numbers (2009-2018 cumulative): \$60.7B in subsidies across rebates, sales tax exemptions, infrastructure subsidies, R\&D, and government procurement. Subsidy per vehicle in 2019: ~¥13,860.

---

## The Overcapacity Problem

- **60+ passenger car manufacturers** in China vs. 20-30 typical in mature markets
- By end of 2023: Chinese EV capacity = **116% of worldwide demand** (Goldman Sachs)
- Companies planned to expand capacity by a further 18% in 2024 to 17 million units — 3 million more than global demand
- Pattern mirrors China's solar and steel industries: state-driven growth → rapid expansion and market dominance → overcapacity → price wars → profitability collapse as subsidies fade

---

## BYD: Cost Control Through Vertical Integration

BYD's competitive advantage: managing the entire production chain in-house — raw materials, battery cells, electronic control systems. This reduces reliance on external suppliers, minimises costs, and mitigates supply chain risk. Goldman Sachs study: Chinese automaker costs are **47% lower** than global peers. Economies of scale (high facility utilisation) critical in this capital-intensive industry.

---

## Tesla's China Advantage (Illustrating the Subsidy System)

Tesla built its Shanghai gigafactory in **one year** (vs. ~two years for Berlin, delayed by environmental concerns). Received:

- \$521M in loans from Chinese state banks at favourable rates
- \$82M in grant funding
- 15% corporate income tax rate (2019-2023) vs. standard 25%

---

## Trade Tensions and Tariffs

President Trump's escalating tariff structure on Chinese goods:

- 25% tariff on Canada/Mexico imports (broader trade stance)
- Additional 20% tariff on Chinese imports (fentanyl justification)
- "Liberation Day" (April 2, 2025): additional 34% tariff on Chinese imports
- **Total proposed tariff on Chinese EVs: 154%** — effectively shutting out the US market

The EU has also been imposing tariffs on Chinese EVs (referenced in _A Change of Pace_, Apr 2024 re: German auto threats). Within China, ICE car sales contracted by **17.4% in 2024** as domestic EV adoption accelerated.

---

## The Solar/Steel Analogy

GQG draws an explicit parallel to China's previous cycles in solar panels and steel:

1. Government subsidies trigger rapid expansion
2. Domestic market dominance achieved
3. Overcapacity floods global markets, drives down prices and profitability
4. Global trading partners erect barriers
5. Without sustained innovation or export markets, boom gives way to bust

The question for Chinese EVs: can they find enough export markets to absorb excess capacity before financial stress hits the weaker producers? GQG's view: the structural pressures are building and the trajectory is familiar.

## ⚠️ Cross-Reference: Contradictions with Prior Views
