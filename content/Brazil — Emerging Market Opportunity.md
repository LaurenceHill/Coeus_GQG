# Brazil — Emerging Market Opportunity

**Source:** Awakening the Sleeping Giant | GQG Research (Nov 2023)
**Tags:** #brazil #emerging-markets #reform #Lula #macro #SOE

---

## Summary

GQG argued in November 2023 that Brazil's structural reforms of 2016–2022 were profoundly underappreciated by global investors. Having survived hyperinflation, a boom, a corruption scandal (Car Wash), and a lost decade, Brazil emerged with an independently run central bank, a reformed pension system, privatised SOEs, and tamed inflation — setting up what GQG viewed as one of the most compelling emerging market investment opportunities.

---

## Three Periods of Brazilian Economic History

### Period 1: 1990s Hyperinflation and the Plano Real

Four-digit annual inflation was routine in the early 1990s. President Cardoso's Plano Real (as Finance Minister, then President 1994–2002) crushed inflation from ~2,500% in 1993 to ~2% by 1998. Cardoso also launched a major privatisation programme, including Vale (the world's largest iron ore miner) in a landmark 1997 IPO.

### Period 2: 2000s — The Lula Boom

Lula da Silva (elected 2002) surprised markets by continuing Cardoso's orthodox economic policies despite his socialist background. His famous principle: _"I'm not ashamed to change my mind. I prefer to be a walking metamorphosis and change whenever the facts change."_ Combined with a commodity supercycle, Brazil became one of the world's best-performing stock markets. Brazil's foreign reserves grew ~10x to \$300 billion. Over 20 million Brazilians lifted out of poverty.

### Period 3: 2010s — Car Wash Scandal and Recovery

Lula's successor Dilma Rousseff abandoned orthodoxy: subsidised lending, frozen prices, SOE interference, and Brazil's development bank (BNDES) briefly exceeded the World Bank in size. Commodity collapse in 2014 and the Lava Jato (Car Wash) corruption scandal — centred on Petrobras — caused Brazil's sovereign debt to be downgraded to junk. GDP per capita still ~30% below 2011 levels a decade later.

**The Silver Lining:** Crisis forced a return to pragmatism.

## 2016–2022 Reform Programme (The Underappreciated Leg)

GQG argues this reform programme was among the most ambitious in all emerging markets:

**SOE Reform**

- 2016 law protecting SOE minority shareholders from political interference
- Petrobras: near-bankrupt under Dilma → hired professional executives, cleaned balance sheet, divested non-core assets → made as much money as Chevron (3x the market cap) by 2023; paid ~$60B in dividends since 2021 on a $100B market cap
- Privatised Eletrobras (largest power utility in Latin America) and dozens of other SOEs

**Fiscal Reform**

- 2019 landmark pension reform: minimum retirement age introduced, worker contributions increased — expected to save ~\$200B. Brazil's pension had been ~45% of the federal budget.

**Monetary Independence**

- Central bank gained formal independence from the executive in 2021
- Raised rates 1,200 bps within 18 months post-COVID inflation spike → inflation collapsed → began cutting rates well ahead of developed markets

## Investment Case (Nov 2023)

- Economy beat market expectations for 3 consecutive years despite rates rising from 2% to ~14%
- Reforms appear systematically under-priced by global investors
- Petrobras as a case study of SOE transformation
- Attractive valuations relative to reform trajectory

---

## ⚠️ Risks

- Lula returned to the presidency in 2022 (his third term) — GQG's bullish case rested on the institutional reforms surviving political cycles; this is the key risk to monitor
- Commodity-dependent economy; external shock vulnerability
- SOE interference risk remains despite legislative protections
