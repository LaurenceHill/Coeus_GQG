# European Policy Regression — When DM Becomes EM

**Source:** When Developed Markets Become Emerging Markets | GQG Research (Feb 2024)
**Tags:** #europe #emerging-markets #DM #policy-risk #energy #nationalisation #de-industrialisation #geopolitics

---

## Summary

February 2024 paper examining how Europe — once the paradigm of developed-market stability — had, through a sequence of policy errors and external shocks, begun exhibiting the hallmarks of emerging market risk: energy insecurity, state confiscation of private assets, arbitrary windfall taxes, fiscal deterioration, currency weakness, and accelerating industrial decline. GQG uses this analysis to justify their persistent underweight in European equities and their preference for genuine EM reform stories (India, Brazil, Indonesia) over European "safety." The paper inverts the traditional developed/emerging framing: political and policy risk is not a developing world monopoly.

---

## The Core Thesis

The conventional assumption is that "developed market" status implies:
- Secure property rights
- Predictable regulatory frameworks
- Stable energy infrastructure
- Fiscal discipline
- Strong currency

GQG's paper argues that Europe had violated each of these assumptions in the 2022–2024 period. The *label* of "developed market" had become misleading — investors were paying a premium for stability that no longer existed.

---

## Energy Insecurity

**The structural failure:**
- Europe had allowed itself to become ~**40% dependent on Russian natural gas** prior to the Ukraine invasion (Feb 2022)
- This was not a sudden shock — the dependence had been built over decades, with warnings ignored
- When the tap was turned off, Europe faced an energy price crisis that rivalled the 1970s oil shock in its economic impact

**The industrial consequence:**
- EU electricity demand fell **~3% in H1 2023** — not from efficiency gains, but from demand destruction: energy-intensive industries curtailing or relocating production
- **De-industrialisation** is GQG's term — the unwinding of Europe's industrial base due to uncompetitive energy costs, a process that is not easily reversed once facilities close or relocate

**The renewable gap:**
- UK offshore wind auction **failed in September 2023**: no bids received at the government's strike price cap — evidence that the energy transition was proceeding slower than political rhetoric implied
- Europe was caught between an old energy system it had abandoned and a new one it had not yet built

## Nationalisations

GQG documents outright state seizures of private energy infrastructure:

| Company | Action | Cost |
|---|---|---|
| **Uniper** (Germany) | Nationalised after Russian gas cut-off | ~**€15 billion** to government |
| **EDF** (France) | Re-nationalised (Macron government) | Full renationalisation of partially privatised utility |

These are not emerging market anecdotes — they are the largest European energy companies. The implicit contract between European governments and private capital (predictable rules, respect for property rights) was broken under crisis conditions. GQG's argument: if it can happen to Uniper, it can happen to any sector deemed "strategically important" in the next crisis.

---

## Windfall Taxes

The energy crisis triggered a wave of retroactive taxation across Europe — taxing energy companies on profits that arose from circumstances beyond their control:

| Country | Windfall Tax Rate |
|---|---|
| Netherlands | ~90% |
| Germany | ~90% |
| France | ~90% |
| Norway | ~35% (on petroleum profits) |
| United Kingdom | ~45% |

**GQG's framing:** These tax rates are structurally incompatible with the investment GQG needs from energy companies to fund the transition to cleaner energy. A company facing 90% windfall taxation cannot invest in new capacity at the rate required. The policy is self-defeating: it taxes the profits needed to fund the very infrastructure Europe needs.

More broadly, retroactive windfall taxes — applied *after* profits were earned, to rates not in force when investment decisions were made — are classic emerging market behaviour. They undermine the long-term credibility of any regulatory framework.

---

## Fiscal Deterioration

European fiscal positions deteriorated sharply in the 2022–2024 period:

- **Fiscal deficits ranged from 3–7% of GDP** across major European economies
- Germany broke its own constitutional debt brake
- France's fiscal position deteriorated faster than expected, prompting sovereign rating pressure
- The EU's own fiscal rules had been suspended since COVID and remained effectively dormant

This is not a Maastricht-era Europe of fiscal constraint — it is a Europe running structural deficits during an inflationary period, which ordinarily warrants tightening.

---

## Currency Weakness

A tangible market signal of Europe's declining relative attractiveness:

- The **EUR weakened against the Brazilian Real (BRL) and Mexican Peso (MXN)** over the period covered
- This is the inverse of what "safe haven" developed market status implies
- Genuine EM reform stories (Brazil, Mexico) were delivering better fiscal management and institutional credibility than the supposed safe havens in Western Europe

---

## The Tech Sector Contrast

GQG draws a sharp contrast between Europe and EM peers on technology:

**Europe's failure:**
- Europe's tech regulatory framework — GDPR (2018), Digital Services Act, AI Act — created enormous compliance costs and legal uncertainty for technology companies
- No globally competitive European technology platform has emerged since the mid-2010s
- European tech regulation is often framed as principled privacy protection; GQG's framing is that it effectively killed the sector's ability to compete with US and Chinese hyperscalers

**EM peers doing better:**
- **India:** Massive domestic tech sector (Infosys, TCS, Wipro) plus a thriving startup ecosystem; open to foreign tech investment
- **Brazil:** Nubank as a genuinely world-class fintech challenger; regulatory environment supportive of digital banking competition
- **Indonesia:** Large young population, growing digital economy; competitive fintech and e-commerce sector

The irony: the markets traditionally labelled "risky" had more vibrant, competitive technology sectors than the "safe" developed market of Europe.

## GQG's Portfolio Implications

The paper justifies:
1. **Underweight Europe** relative to benchmark — particularly energy companies facing windfall tax risk, utilities facing re-nationalisation risk, and industrials facing energy cost headwinds
2. **Overweight selective EM** — India, Brazil, and Indonesia as beneficiaries of capital that would otherwise have gone to Europe
3. **Scepticism of the "European value" narrative** — cheap European stocks may be cheap for structural reasons, not as cyclical opportunities

**Where GQG remained selectively constructive on Europe:** The [[European Opportunity — Banking and Energy]] page (from *A Spirited Debate*, May 2024) shows that European banks and specific energy names remained in the portfolio — GQG distinguished between *European policy risk broadly* and *specific companies trading at deep discounts with improving fundamentals*. This paper (Feb 2024) is the framework for why the bar is high; the May 2024 paper shows where specific stocks cleared that bar.

## ⚠️ Tension with European Opportunity — Banking and Energy

There is an apparent tension between this paper (Feb 2024 — bearish on European policy environment) and [[European Opportunity — Banking and Energy]] (May 2024 — selectively bullish on European banks and energy names). These are not contradictory:

- This paper establishes the **structural framework**: European policy is deteriorating and the "DM premium" is not warranted
- The banking/energy paper applies **stock-level selectivity**: within a difficult environment, specific names trading at extreme discounts could still outperform — *less bad* is good, and you don't need Europe to fix itself, just for banks to earn their cost of capital
- GQG's posture: avoid European equities as an asset class, but hold specific names where the individual stock thesis is compelling on fundamentals
