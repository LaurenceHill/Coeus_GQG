# Defensive Sectors — 2026 Opportunity

**Sources:** Against the Grain | CIO Perspectives (Jan 2026); Is Defensive the New Offensive? | GQG Research (Feb 2026); Falling Up | CIO Perspectives (Oct 2024)
**Tags:** #defensive #utilities #staples #insurance #valuation #opportunity #2026 #coiled-spring

---

## Summary

As of January–February 2026, GQG identified historically defensive sectors — utilities, consumer staples, healthcare, and insurance — as offering exceptional risk-reward. The market's laser focus on AI/tech had left these sectors near multi-decade lows in relative P/E and benchmark weight, while their underlying fundamentals were improving. GQG viewed these as "the unloved" — a natural rotation target after cutting AI exposure. The February 2026 paper (_Is Defensive the New Offensive?_) formalised this into a quantitative "coiled spring" thesis with nearly 40 years of empirical support. The rotation itself began in Q3 2024 (see [[Falling Up — Q3 2024 CIO Letter]]).

---

## The Setup

A narrative-driven market that prices AI stocks at extreme multiples simultaneously creates the mirror image: boring, durable businesses become deeply undervalued in relative terms. GQG's portfolio rotated _into_ this dislocation.

**Key metrics cited (Jan 2026):**

- Defensive sectors at or near **all-time lows in relative P/E vs. S\&P 500**
- Defensive sector benchmark weight near **all-time lows**
- Weighted average EPS growth for GQG strategies: ~high single digits + 3-4% dividend yield
- Average portfolio P/E: **mid-teens or lower**

## Sector-by-Sector Analysis

### Utilities

GQG cited US utilities as positioned to grow earnings **faster, more predictably, and with more long-term visibility than at any point in the past 20+ years**, while trading at a discount to the S\&P 500.

Key example: **American Water Works**

- \~8% earnings growth, regardless of economic environment (beta: 0.2x)
- Previously traded at a _premium_ to the S\&P 500 (faster growth, lower risk)
- By Jan 2026: trading at a **discount** (21x vs. S\&P 500's 22x)
- Estimated return profile: low double-digit total return (high single-digit EPS + 3% dividend yield) with less risk than the index
- Deliberately avoided utilities whose growth thesis depended on AI datacenter load growth — only those with genuine structural demand tailwinds

### Consumer Staples

Many staples companies were trading at "significant discounts to the S\&P 500, which we think was previously unimaginable."

Key example: **Philip Morris International**

- 19x EPS — meaningful discount to S\&P 500
- Reinvented via next-generation (reduced-risk) products; rapid growth
- Estimated return: mid-teens total return (low double-digit EPS + 4% dividend yield)
- GQG's view: Philip Morris may deliver **faster earnings growth than most of the Magnificent 7 over the next five years**

Second example: **Heineken**

- 13x earnings
- Mid- to high-single digit earnings growth potential
- Emerging markets footprint (beer consumption still growing globally — with or without GLP-1s)
- COVID-era price digestion period ending; no structural issues, in GQG's view
- Family-owned business; strong operators

_(Note: Heineken here is viewed constructively at 13x — contrast with cautious sector tone in [[Alcohol Industry Structural Decline]] from 2024, though that paper focused on spirits, not beer)_

### Insurance and Healthcare

Cited as offering attractive total return potential alongside utilities and staples. Less detailed analysis in this paper — likely covered in other GQG publications.

## The "Coiled Spring" — Quantitative Case (Feb 2026)

_Is Defensive the New Offensive?_ (Feb 2026) provides the empirical architecture for this view:

**The benchmark weight gap:**

- Defensives currently represent **~17% of S\&P 500** vs. a **26% long-term average** — a 9-point gap
- Empirically, every **1% change in defensives' share** of the S\&P 500 has historically aligned with roughly **10% of relative performance**
- A 9% gap implies **~90% potential relative upside** if composition reverts to long-term average
- GQG does not underwrite this outcome, but views it as optionality on top of fundamentals that already meet their hurdle rate

**Index concentration:**

- \~**50% of S\&P 500 market cap** is tied to AI-related exposure
- The "Mag 7" alone represent ~**35% of the index**
- This is the post-ChatGPT (Nov 2022) rally reshaping the index — widening the valuation gap to levels rarely seen

**Dotcom parallel:**

- During the dotcom crash (S\&P 500 peak-to-trough: -49%), consumer staples delivered **11.2% annualized returns** — approximately **33 percentage points of annual outperformance**
- Utilities as a basket: **nearly 50% total returns** over the same dotcom crash period
- Altria surged **100%+** from March 2000 (the market peak) through December 2000
- The S\&P 500 took **7 years to recover**; the Nasdaq took **15 years**; defensive stocks had minimal recovery periods because they had minimal losses

**Case studies that met all four criteria simultaneously** (>500% total return, ≥10% compounded EPS growth, <15x P/E, ≥20 years of consecutive dividend maintenance/growth):

- **Kroger** and **Allstate** — not any of the Magnificent Seven
- Note: each of the "gilded tech names" met at least one criterion but none met all four simultaneously

## The Broader Argument

This is not a macro call. GQG explicitly states they were not predicting a recession or bear market. The opportunity arose purely from **valuation dislocation** — the market's preference for narrative drove capital away from boring compounders, creating a rare entry point.

> _"Why even bother guessing who will win the AI wars when you can get better growth from a boring tobacco company?"_

> _"Ask a group of people to compare the perceived value of ChatGPT to auto insurance. While most people pay for — and would pay more for — auto insurance, very few pay for ChatGPT, and far fewer would pay more if its price went up."_
