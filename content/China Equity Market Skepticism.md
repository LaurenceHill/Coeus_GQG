# China Equity Market Skepticism

**Sources:** Beyond the Rally | GQG Research (Mar 2025); The Chinese Government's Stranglehold on Private Enterprises | GQG Research (Apr 2023)
**Cross-reference:** A Change of Pace | CIO Perspectives (Apr 2024) — EM ex-China bullish stance
**Tags:** #china #emerging-markets #equities #geopolitics #SOE #valuation

---

## Summary

GQG's March 2025 paper made a comprehensive case for why China equities remain uninvestable for long-term investors: 30 years of near-zero cumulative returns, earnings growth that has failed to materialise despite extraordinary economic growth, escalating geopolitical tensions, and no structural catalyst for change. GQG views China as "an interesting trade at best" rather than a long-term allocation.

---

## The Core Empirical Problem

> *"$1 invested in MSCI China at the beginning of 1993 would be worth (excluding dividends) roughly $0.80 today, in nominal terms."*

Over every starting year from 1993 through ~2021, buying MSCI China and holding produced negative excess returns vs. MSCI ACWI, with a **median outcome of approximately -600 bps per annum**. Only three vintages yielded positive excess returns — and even those didn't exceed +20-40 bps per year before adjusting for risk.

The paradox: China's economy was the strongest growth story on earth from the mid-1990s through the GFC — per capita GDP rose ~6x vs. less than 2x globally — yet shareholders were not rewarded. Why?
- State-owned enterprises funnelled cash into state priorities rather than dividends
- Private firms faced "regulatory whipsaws" (the "common prosperity" crackdown on tech giants)
- Governance structures systematically favoured state objectives over shareholder returns

---

## The "Sisyphean Trade"

GQG coined this metaphor: investing in China feels like Sisyphus pushing a boulder up a hill — just when you think you've reached the top (each rally), you roll back down. The March 2025 rally felt like "Sisyphus approaching the top of the hill again."

---

## Structural Headwinds Going Forward

GQG argues China's **most challenging years lie ahead**, not behind:
1. **Demographics**: Ageing population, shrinking working-age cohort
2. **Property market**: Ongoing structural deflation, unresolved developer debt crisis
3. **State interventions**: Persistent and unpredictable; "common prosperity" regulatory risk
4. **Geopolitical deterioration**: US-China relations described as "Cold War-type" — and worsening
5. **Fiscal dependency**: China's 2025 fiscal stimulus expected at near-pandemic levels — a sign of stress, not confidence

---

## The Geopolitical Dimension

GQG quoted directly from the White House's "America First Investment Policy" memo, which listed China (including Hong Kong and Macau) as a "Foreign Adversary" alongside Cuba, Iran, North Korea, Russia, and Venezuela — and explicitly cited China's Military-Civil Fusion strategy, which compels civilian companies to support military activities. GQG: *"We feel we would be dismissive if we did not worry about the potential implications."*

---

## Valuation Argument — Even If You Ignore Geopolitics

Over the last decade, cumulative corporate earnings growth in China has been **negative in USD terms**, yet valuations trade "in line to slightly richer" than 10 years ago. GQG asks: what should one pay for a "growthless perpetuity" that must be discounted at an increasingly aggressive rate to compensate for geopolitical and domestic risk? Conclusion: it doesn't screen as a bargain even on a pure valuation basis.

---

## GQG's Posture

Described as sidestepping China in their Emerging Markets allocation ("in our recent paper, Turning Tides in Emerging Markets, we sidestepped discussing China, as it did not align with the more optimistic views we have for most emerging market countries"). They are not short China but regard it as uninvestable on a 3-5 year view.

---

## ⚠️ Contrast with Brazil

Both China and Brazil have significant [[SOE Reform and Corporate Governance|SOE problems]]. The difference: Brazil underwent a genuine, market-friendly reform programme 2016–2022 ([[Brazil — Emerging Market Opportunity]]). China's SOE system remains structurally subordinate to party objectives with no equivalent reform trajectory.

---

## ⚠️ Contradiction Note

*A Change of Pace* (Apr 2024) cited GQG's bullish stance on Emerging Markets including Brazil and India while "excluding China." *Beyond the Rally* (Mar 2025) formalises this exclusion with a detailed bear case — consistent in direction, deepening the conviction.
