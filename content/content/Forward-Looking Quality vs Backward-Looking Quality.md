# Forward-Looking Quality vs Backward-Looking Quality

**Source:** Dotcom on Steroids | GQG Research (Sep 2025)
**Tags:** #investment-framework #quality #GQG #mental-model #tech #forward-looking

---

## Summary

GQG's central analytical framework for evaluating business quality over time. "Forward-Looking Quality" — the ability to sustain and grow earnings durably into the future — is what GQG seeks. "Backward-Looking Quality" describes companies whose quality was genuine but is now diminishing due to market saturation, increased competition, or capital intensity. The Dotcom on Steroids series marked the first time in GQG's history they declared the majority of large technology companies to be **Backward-Looking**.

---

## The Framework

### Forward-Looking Quality

Characteristics:

- Durable, growing earnings power with a long visible runway
- Competitive advantages that compound (network effects, switching costs, pricing power)
- Capital efficiency improving or stable
- Addressable market still large relative to current penetration
- Management aligned with long-term value creation

Examples in GQG portfolios at various times: NVIDIA (2022–2024), Eli Lilly (GLP-1 runway), Petrobras post-reform, utility compounders (American Water Works), Philip Morris (next-gen product reinvention)

### Backward-Looking Quality

Characteristics:

- Quality was real but peak dominance has been reached
- Revenue growth structurally decelerating
- Increasing capital expenditure per unit of revenue
- Free cash flow margins deteriorating
- Competition eroding moats (e.g., open-source vs. closed-source AI)
- Market expectations still pricing in forward-looking growth that won't materialise

Examples flagged (Sep 2025): Major tech companies with dominant market positions but slowing growth — Microsoft (~60% of software profits), Nvidia (~60% of semiconductor profits at the time). At maximum market share, the growth ceiling becomes an arithmetical constraint.

---

## Why the Distinction Matters for Valuation

High multiples are only justified by high _future_ earnings growth. When a company's quality is backward-looking:

- The market pays a forward-looking multiple for a backward-looking growth rate
- The multiple must compress (see [[Multiple Compression Risk]])
- The compression can be violent because the high starting multiple magnifies the price decline

Howard Marks, quoted by GQG: _"There are no bad assets, only bad prices."_ The reverse: there are no perpetually good assets at any price. Even Microsoft and Amazon — clearly great businesses — lost 33% and 80% respectively within months at the dotcom peak.

---

## Application to Big Tech (2025)

| Company | Historical Quality | Forward-Looking Issue |
|---|---|---|
| Microsoft | Genuine monopoly in enterprise software | ~60% of software profits → growth ceiling; CapEx as % of revenue rising |
| Nvidia | Real hardware dominance | ~60-80% GPU market share → size makes next-leg harder; cyclicality risk |
| Apple | Ecosystem stickiness | P/E above Nvidia (Jan 2025) with far lower growth rate — more price than earnings |
| Alphabet/Amazon | Cloud platform dominance | CapEx % of revenue rising sharply; revenue growth decelerating to mid-single digits |

GQG's explicit note: "These are names we have owned in the past in a big way — and very well may own in the future when the visibility improves — but at the current juncture we remain cautious."

---

## The Quality Signal That Works in Reverse

GQG's confidence in the "backward-looking" call rested on a structural pattern they'd seen before:

- Once a company controls 60%+ of an industry's profits, the organic growth runway narrows sharply
- Price-to-earnings ratios detach from earnings reality during euphoric phases
- The eventual re-rating is not a function of bad management but of arithmetic: you cannot compound a large base at the same rate as a small one

This is the same logic GQG applied to [[Alcohol Industry Structural Decline]] (market leaders who priced too aggressively, now facing structural volume decline) and to [[China Equity Market Skepticism]] (economic growth that didn't translate into shareholder returns due to structural governance issues).
