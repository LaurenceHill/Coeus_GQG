# OpenAI — Business Model Analysis

**Source:** Dotcom on Steroids: Part II | GQG Research (Nov 2025)
**Tags:** #OpenAI #AI #bubble #valuation #AGI #commoditisation #private-markets

---

## Summary

GQG's November 2025 deep-dive concluded that OpenAI — while genuinely innovative — is one of the most overvalued and overhyped companies in history. The gap between its narrative (AGI pioneer, world's most valuable startup) and its economics (no moat, commoditising product, negative FCF, circular financing) is the starkest GQG has seen. More importantly, because OpenAI sits at the centre of a web of circular financing relationships across large public tech companies, its valuation collapse would have systemic spillover effects.

---

## The Numbers

**Revenue:** ~\$20B annualised run-rate (as of Nov 2025)

**Valuation:** Hundreds of billions — GQG compares unfavourably to Amazon and Google at equivalent valuations:

- Amazon at $500B valuation: revenues ~10x larger than OpenAI's current $20B
- Google at \$500B valuation: revenues ~4x larger

**Free Cash Flow:** Large and widening negative outflows. 2025 FCF projections show accelerating cash burn as cloud computing and data centre costs surge. Capital infusions from Microsoft (and others) have been "record-breaking."

**Compute costs:** CapEx surging; OpenAI is developing chips in-house (partly forced by US export controls limiting access to cutting-edge NVIDIA hardware for training) — a strategy that adds capital intensity without the manufacturing scale to be efficient.

---

## The Moat Problem

Four structural weaknesses GQG identified:

**1. Hallucinations and reliability gaps**
LLMs continue to produce unreliable outputs. The workaround (re-runs, human-in-the-loop verification) directly undermines the core value proposition of efficiency. Makes embedding in mission-critical applications slow and risky.

**2. Low switching costs**
28% of OpenAI's API usage flows through low-code platforms (Zapier, Bubble, Retool) that are model-agnostic. Enterprise customers can redirect workloads to cheaper or better models in days. This is the opposite of a sticky business.

**3. Commoditisation from open-source**
DeepSeek, Meta's Llama, and other open-source models are reaching efficacy comparable to closed-source offerings. Commoditisation lowers switching costs further, erodes pricing power, and removes the ability to charge premium rates.

**4. The token economics trap**
The cost per _token_ has plummeted due to hardware and software advances. But the cost per _query_ has not fallen at the same rate — because reasoning models require far more tokens per task. Simple single-turn queries fell from 80% of enterprise usage (early 2024) to just 20% by year-end; replaced by multi-step complex workflows generating far more output tokens (which are priced higher). The more capable the model, the more tokens consumed. "The cheaper it gets, the more you pay."

## The Metric Problem

OpenAI reports 800M weekly active users — impressive, but:

- \~90% of ChatGPT users are outside the US (per OpenAI's own CFO)
- A large proportion of engagement is from free-tier users using it for non-productive purposes
- Free-tier usage inflates metrics used to raise capital but has low signalling power for sustainable revenue

**Benchmark gaming:** AI models have reportedly improved benchmark scores partly by "memorising" test questions. Benchmarks measure generalist breadth; enterprises need specialist consistency. This creates a conceptual mismatch between what gets measured and what matters.

## The Systemic Risk: Circular Financing

OpenAI is not just a single company risk. It sits at the centre of a circular financing web:

- Microsoft, Google, and others have invested billions in OpenAI
- These same companies are also OpenAI's largest cloud customers (providing much of OpenAI's revenue)
- OpenAI's valuation, in turn, supports the narrative and valuations of its investor-partners
- OpenAI announces new "partnership" deals periodically — each announcement briefly props up the valuations of its tech partners

GQG's conclusion: OpenAI is "helping to fuel the AI bubble" through its role in driving the "hopes and dreams" factor. If capital markets begin to doubt that AGI is attainable within a reasonable timeframe — or if funding dries up — the spillover effects across the entire AI supply chain and the companies intertwined with OpenAI "could be massive."

**Key quote from OpenAI CFO Sarah Friar** (at an AI conference in Miami, attended by GQG): The company's "true north" is "AGI for the benefit of humanity." GQG's interpretation: the business is being run as a mission, not a profit-maximising enterprise — a fragile foundation for the valuations it props up.

## ⚠️ Context: This Is a Private Company

GQG acknowledges they typically ignore private companies. They broke this rule for OpenAI because its financing relationships directly affect the public companies in their investable universe. The analysis of OpenAI is therefore an indirect analysis of Microsoft, Google, Amazon, and others.
