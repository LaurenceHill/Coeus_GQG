# SOE Reform and Corporate Governance

**Sources:** Awakening the Sleeping Giant (Nov 2023); Beyond the Rally (Mar 2025); China's Electric Dream (Apr 2025); The Chinese Government's Stranglehold on Private Enterprises (Apr 2023)
**Tags:** #SOE #governance #corporate #reform #brazil #china #emerging-markets

---

## Summary

State-owned enterprises (SOEs) are a recurring theme across GQG's EM analysis. Poorly governed SOEs systematically destroy shareholder value by subordinating commercial objectives to political goals. The key analytical distinction GQG draws: markets where SOE reform has genuinely occurred (Brazil) vs. markets where it hasn't (China). Reform trajectory — not current SOE quality — is the critical investment variable.

---

## Why SOEs Destroy Value

- Political meddling in management and capital allocation decisions
- Cash funnelled to state priorities rather than dividends or reinvestment in productive assets
- Crowd out private sector investment (more efficient capital allocators)
- Create concentration risk: when a country's largest companies are SOEs, corporate governance problems are systemic

---

## Brazil: Reform That Worked

After the Car Wash Scandal (centred on Petrobras), Brazil enacted genuine SOE reform:

- **2016 law**: Protected SOE minority shareholders from political interference
- **Petrobras case study**: Near-bankrupt under Dilma's mismanagement → hired professional executives, cleaned balance sheet, divested non-core assets, improved governance → made as much as Chevron (3x the market cap) by 2023; paid ~\$60B in dividends since 2021
- **Privatisations**: Dozens of SOEs sold, including Eletrobras (largest LatAm power utility) and Vale (world's largest iron ore miner — privatised 1997)

See: [[Brazil — Emerging Market Opportunity]]

---

## China: Reform That Hasn't

China's SOE system remains structurally subordinate to party objectives:

- State enterprises raised billions via IPOs/secondary offerings (1990-2010) but paid minimal dividends
- Cash retained for "operational bloat" or state priorities
- Tech giants subjected to "common prosperity" regulatory crackdowns when they grew too powerful
- Military-Civil Fusion strategy compels civilian companies (including listed private firms) to support military and intelligence activities
- No structural reform trajectory analogous to Brazil's

The result: China's extraordinary economic growth (per capita GDP ~6x from 1995-2008) produced near-zero cumulative shareholder returns. See: [[China Equity Market Skepticism]]

## China EV Sector: Subsidies as Governance Problem

China's EV industry exemplifies the SOE dynamic even for nominally private firms:

- 60+ manufacturers propped up by state subsidies and mandates
- Overcapacity at 116% of global demand by end of 2023
- Price wars threatening financial stability of weaker players
- Pattern mirrors solar/steel: state-driven boom → profitability destruction → bust

See: [[China EV Industry — Overcapacity and Trade Tensions]]

---

## Investment Implication

GQG's framework: the quality of a country's corporate governance institutions — and the direction of travel on reform — is as important as the country's economic growth rate when assessing long-term equity returns. High growth + poor governance = poor shareholder outcomes (China). High governance reform momentum + improving fundamentals = opportunity (Brazil post-2016).
