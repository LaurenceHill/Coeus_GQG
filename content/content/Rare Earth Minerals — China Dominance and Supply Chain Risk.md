# Rare Earth Minerals — China Dominance and Supply Chain Risk

**Source:** Critical Dependence on Rare-Earth Minerals | GQG Research (Nov 2025)
**Tags:** #rare-earths #china #geopolitics #supply-chain #national-security #minerals #EV #defense

## Summary

China controls >90% of global rare-earth refining and magnet production — not because deposits are uniquely Chinese, but due to decades of deliberate industrial policy, subsidies, and strategic patience. This dominance gives China a potent geopolitical weapon. In 2025, Beijing demonstrated it would use it: export controls on rare-earth magnets temporarily closed Ford's Chicago factory and triggered US supply scrambles. GQG frames this as a structural investment theme around supply chain diversification.

---

## What Are Rare Earths?

17 metals: 15 lanthanides + scandium + yttrium. Despite the name, not geologically scarce — cerium and neodymium are more abundant in the Earth's crust than gold or silver. They are "rare" because economically viable concentrated deposits are uncommon, and processing is technically demanding.

**Why they matter:** Unique magnetic and electronic properties make them irreplaceable in:

- Smartphones and consumer electronics
- Electric vehicles (permanent magnets in motors)
- Wind turbines
- F-35 fighter jets (actuators), Virginia-class submarines
- Tesla's humanoid robots
- Military guidance systems

**Demand forecast:** +60% by 2040 (IEA). Clean energy and EVs are the growth engines.

**Market size:** Annual sales <\$4B — tiny market, outsized strategic importance.

## China's Dominance: How It Happened

**Bayan Obo (Inner Mongolia):** World's largest rare-earth reserve — initially thought to be an iron deposit. Deng Xiaoping, 1992: _"The Middle East has oil; China has rare earths."_

**The VAT strategy (1994):** Export rebates of up to 17% created incentives for massive rare-earth production. Combined with relaxed environmental rules and low labour costs, China's production surged **~500% from 1985 to 1995**, exceeding the rest of the world combined.

**Western retreat:**

- Reagan administration refused to subsidise domestic mineral production; DoD sold >75% of rare-earth stockpiles 1993–2005
- NRC reclassified "nuclear source material" to include >0.05% thorium/uranium → regulations made US deposits uneconomic
- EPA forced closure of Mountain Pass Mine (California) in 1998 — the world's second-largest rare-earth deposit — for "low-level radioactive waste"
- **1995: General Motors sold Magnequench** — once the world's best rare-earth magnet producer — to investors including Chinese firms, transferring technology and IP to China

**China's current position:**

- **>90% of global rare-earth refining**
- **69% of global mining**
- **Monopoly on dysprosium and terbium separation** — critical for high-temperature permanent magnets (F-35 actuators, EVs)
- 39 rare-earth research institutes in China vs. a handful in the US
- China files ~30 rare-earth patents for every 1 filed by the US

## Weaponisation: 2010 and 2025

**2010 Japan embargo:** China halted rare-earth shipments to Japan for two months during a maritime dispute. Rare-earth prices spiked globally. Japan's response: invested in Australia's Lynas Corp., diversified sourcing, built stockpiles, improved recycling. Reduced China dependence from >90% to <60% by 2012.

**WTO ruling (2014):** US, EU, and Japan won a case against China's export restrictions. China dropped restrictions in 2015 — but this "proved to be a disaster in disguise": the resulting price collapse drove Molycorp (Mountain Pass) into bankruptcy and pushed Lynas near collapse. Western competitors couldn't survive without Chinese price support.

**2025 retaliation (Trump tariffs):** After Trump's "Liberation Day" tariffs (April 2025), China:

- Restricted exports of **7 critical rare-earth elements and magnets** (April)
- Added bans on additional metals with **extraterritorial licensing mandates** (October)
- **Ford's Chicago factory shut for weeks** (June) due to magnet supply disruption; US companies scrambled for supply
- **Kuala Lumpur Accord** (late June 2025): both sides agreed to suspend fresh restrictions for one year; US reduced tariffs in exchange

_"Capitalism doesn't work in an industry that is very small and dominated by state capital."_ — Constantine Karayannopoulos, former CEO, Neo Performance Materials

## Investment Implications

**Supply chain diversification plays:**

- Australia's Lynas Corp. (largest rare-earth producer outside China)
- US Mountain Pass (Molycorp successor; MP Materials)
- Japanese investment in overseas REE assets
- Recycling technology developers
- Defence-grade magnet manufacturers outside China

**Risk for companies dependent on Chinese REEs:**

- Automakers (EV motors require rare-earth permanent magnets)
- Defence contractors
- Semiconductor equipment makers
- Wind turbine manufacturers

**The talent gap is structural:** China has 39 REE research institutes vs. a handful in the US; 120 Chinese government-funded REE researchers per 1 US researcher. Catching up will take decades even with policy intent.
