## The Three-Period Reform Story (Jun 2023)

GQG's historical analysis divides India's economic history since independence into three phases:

**Period 1: The Partial Liberalisation (1991)**
Decades of socialism produced a dormant economy — nationalised banking (1970s), tax rates exceeding 90%, customs duties of 200–300%, GDP per capita growing just 1.3%/year. The 1991 foreign exchange crisis forced PM Narasimha Rao to end the "License Raj": state-owned companies privatised, currency peg removed, private investment encouraged. India began to grow. Goldman Sachs named it a BRIC economy in 2001.

**Period 2: Post-GFC Stagnation (2008–2014)**
Politicians forgot what triggered the boom. The reform agenda stalled; corruption scandals emerged (the telecom spectrum scandal being the most prominent). By 2013, Morgan Stanley labelled India one of the **"Fragile Five"** emerging markets.

**Period 3: The Modi Reform Era (2014–present)**
Modi won in 2014 on the "Gujarat model of development" — that state had prioritised infrastructure, cut red tape, and become India's most pro-business and prosperous region. Key reforms delivered at the national level:

| Reform | Year | Impact |
|---|---|---|
| Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code | 2016 | Enforced loan accountability; corporate borrowers can no longer renegotiate from position of strength |
| Real Estate Regulation and Development Act (RERA) | 2016 | Cracked down on fraudulent developers |
| GST Tax Reform | 2017 | Simplified India's tax system; increased government revenue while reducing business burden |
| Corporate tax cut | 2019 | Reduced from 30% to 22% |
| Unified Payments Interface (UPI) | 2016 | Digital payments; brought millions into formal economy; India's payments infrastructure now rivals developed nations |
| Broadband expansion | Ongoing | 12x growth over 10 years to ~900M connections at developing-world prices |
| Air India privatisation | 2022 | Sold to Tata Group; Tata placed India's largest aircraft order one year later |
| National Monetization Pipeline | Ongoing | Monetises state assets; funds infrastructure investment |

GQG's assessment: despite having the world's highest approval ratings by far, Modi's economic transformation is **underappreciated by global markets and mainstream media.** India is the 5th largest stock market ($3.5T) yet represents less than 2% of MSCI ACWI and less than 15% of MSCI EM. GQG attributes negative media coverage partly to the "traditionally champagne-socialist intelligentsia" that lost influence in Delhi post-2014 and airs grievances to foreign correspondents.

## The Adani Situation

### First Test: Hindenburg Report (Late 2023 / Early 2024)
GQG initiated positions across "a variety of Adani companies following a critical short-seller report" in Q4 2023. The short-seller (Hindenburg Research) alleged financial irregularities. GQG's process: focus on whether the underlying businesses are material to India's infrastructure complex — they concluded yes.

### Second Test: US DOJ / SEC Allegations (November 2024)
New allegations surfaced — not from a short-seller but from US government agencies. GQG: "We take these allegations seriously, given their source." But with more information, their view was "essentially unchanged" from a year prior.

**GQG's infrastructure materiality argument:**
- Adani-controlled airports: >20% of all Indian air traffic; ~30% of all air cargo; 50% of all domestic routes
- Adani ports: ~30% of all Indian port capacity; ~3x faster turns than government-owned ports; ~60% of volumes under long-term contracts (up to 30 years)

**The logic:** India cannot build alternative critical infrastructure overnight. These assets are indispensable to the economy's growth trajectory. The legal risks are real but the underlying economic position is durable.

**Process note:** GQG explicitly flagged this as illustrating their commitment to avoiding "knee-jerk reactions to headlines" — one of the explicit expressions of [[GQG Investment Philosophy]].

## GLP-1 and Eli Lilly as the Healthcare Revolution

In the same January 2025 letter, GQG described GLP-1 drugs (Eli Lilly, Novo Nordisk) as the second of "two revolutions" (alongside AI) that were "like freight trains." GLP-1 outcome in 2024:
- Eli Lilly: remained in positive territory for the year
- Novo Nordisk: major detractor in Q4 2024 — stock dropped significantly on relatively modest news (contrast with Apple, which rose despite bad news)

GQG's response: doubled down. Rationale:
- Obesity market is "nascent" — not even close to peak penetration
- Duopoly structure between Lilly and Novo is durable — manufacturing scale and specialised facilities create real barriers
- Both companies can win simultaneously given demand excess over supply

This is consistent with [[GLP-1 Impact on Consumer Industries]] (health benefits expanding) and [[Calibrated Optimism — GQG 2024 Market Stance]] (Eli Lilly as core holding).

## Apple: A Cautionary Valuation Note

GQG used the January 2025 letter to contrast two types of "quality":
- **Nvidia**: price rise driven primarily by earnings; reduced on profit-taking + China sales risk but still a winner
- **Apple**: price rise driven primarily by multiple expansion despite bad news (weak iPhone upgrade cycle, China discounts, Vision Pro production halt)

By January 2025, Apple was trading at a *higher* forward P/E than Nvidia, despite GQG's estimate of less than half the sustainable earnings growth. GQG's verdict: "Apple is a great company, but not so much a great company to own at this time."

This distinction is an early application of the [[Forward-Looking Quality vs Backward-Looking Quality]] framework that was formalised in *Dotcom on Steroids* (Sep 2025).

## Navigating the Herd Mentality — Foreign Flows as Contrarian Signal (Nov 2024)

GQG's November 2024 paper makes a specific empirical case: **foreign investor outflows from India are a contrarian buying signal, not a reason to sell.**

Historical data (2005–2024, monthly net foreign investment bucketed into quintiles vs. 12-month forward relative performance vs. EM benchmarks):
- **Bottom 20%** of months by foreign inflows (= highest outflow months) → **best median forward 12-month relative returns**
- **Top 20%** of months by inflows → **weakest forward returns** (over-crowded, over-priced)

**October 2024 data point:** Among India's largest monthly net foreign outflows ever recorded — which GQG framed as a **contrarian buy signal**.

**2022 precedent:** Record foreign outflows of $20B+ in secondary markets in 2022 preceded strong 12-month returns.

GQG's logic: as fellow foreign investors, they view their cohort's selling "as a contrarian signal to buy." The herd mentality of foreign institutionals consistently overreacts to short-term sentiment shifts.

**Mispriced sectors (Nov 2024):** Infrastructure and utilities — sectors with **lower-than-average foreign institutional ownership** despite robust domestic earnings growth. GQG views low foreign ownership as a signal of mispricing, not structural risk.

**NIFTY fundamental case (Nov 2024):**
- NIFTY EPS growth has materially outpaced both MSCI EM and MSCI ACWI ex-US over 5 years
- Valuations broadly stable — performance driven by earnings growth, not multiple expansion
- India macro: favourable GDP growth, positive fiscal outlook, strong demographic tailwinds
