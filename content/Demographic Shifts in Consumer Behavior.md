# Demographic Shifts in Consumer Behavior

**Source:** A Spirited Debate | GQG Research (May 2024)
**Tags:** #demographics #consumer #health #structural #alcohol #GLP-1

---

## Summary

Younger generations in developed markets are changing consumption behaviors — drinking less, prioritizing health, and showing different brand loyalties. GQG identifies this as a structural, not cyclical, force with multi-decade implications for alcohol, and potentially other consumer staples industries.

---

## Explanation

### The Alcohol Data Point

From Gallup polling:

- 18-34 year olds in the US who drink: **72% two decades ago → 62% in 2023**
- Among those who still drink: frequency is down, occasion volume is down
- **52% now believe even moderate drinking is unhealthy** — up from 34% five years earlier

This is a self-reinforcing trend: as the scientific consensus on alcohol's health harms strengthens, attitudes shift, and the shift among young cohorts affects lifetime consumption trajectories. This cohort will age — carrying these habits with them.

### Why This Is Structural, Not Cyclical

Health beliefs, once formed, are sticky. Scientific consensus on alcohol's harms (from the WHO, cancer research bodies, and others) has been strengthening for years. Unlike a recession where spending recovers, a consumer who concludes that drinking is unhealthy does not "recover" that behavior.

### Implications for Industry Analysis

Traditional industry framing: "people drink less but better" — implying positive mix even on falling volumes. GQG is skeptical that premiumization can offset enough of the volume decline, particularly when:

1. The heaviest drinkers (who drive most revenue) are a concentrated, aging cohort
2. Younger cohorts entering their prime consumption years are less likely to drink at all
3. The trend is global and driven by health awareness, not just economic conditions

### Broader Consumer Applicability

The same demographic health-consciousness is relevant to:

- Fast food / ultra-processed food (implicitly threatened by similar health awareness shifts)
- Tobacco (already well advanced in its structural decline)
- GLP-1 drugs, which structurally alter appetite and addiction-linked behaviors ([[GLP-1 Impact on Consumer Industries]])
