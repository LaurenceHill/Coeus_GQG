## Summary

A recurring backdrop across GQG's energy and industrial papers. The Paris Agreement target of limiting warming to 2°C by 2050 drives decarbonisation policy globally, but GQG's analysis consistently focuses on the _investment implications_ of the transition rather than the policy goals themselves — identifying which companies are best positioned to profit from decarbonisation vs. which carry stranded asset risk.

---

## Key Themes Already Captured

**Carbon removal:**
The IPCC AR6 requires ~10 GT of CO₂ captured per year for net zero by 2050. Current global CCS capacity is ~44 mtpa — a ~225x gap. Closing it represents a massive investment opportunity for companies with existing infrastructure ([[Carbon Capture and Sequestration]]).

**Power demand paradox:**
The energy transition is itself energy-intensive. Electrification of transport and industry, plus the intermittency of renewables (requiring 3x the capacity of fossil generation), means total electricity demand is rising sharply even as the _mix_ shifts to clean sources ([[US Power Demand Surge]]).

**State-driven transition risk:**
Government mandates and subsidies can create overcapacity and boom-bust dynamics. China's EV sector shows how aggressive state support creates structural distortions ([[China EV Industry — Overcapacity and Trade Tensions]]).

**IRA Policy Tailwinds (US):**
The Inflation Reduction Act dramatically improved the economics of CCS (\$180/MT for DAC), renewables, and clean energy infrastructure. Under the Trump administration (from Jan 2025), some of these incentives face policy uncertainty — a potential stale claim in the Carbon Capture paper which was written under Biden-era assumptions.

## GQG's Core Thesis: Net Zero 2050 May Be an Illusion (Feb 2023)

GQG's most direct engagement with the Net Zero debate argues the target is **incompatible with continuous economic growth and current consumption patterns** unless major societal behaviour changes occur — changes that are politically unpopular and largely absent from policy discourse.

> _"The belief that we can completely remove the amount of greenhouse gas emissions produced by human activity in less than three decades without major societal behavior changes seems to be questionable — if not a deceiving notion."_

### The Scale of the Problem

- US carbon footprint: **16 tons/person/year** — one of the world's highest
- EU: 6.8 tons/person/year; Global average: ~4 tons
- Required by 2050: **below 2 tons/person/year** globally
- IRA commitment: \$370B toward 40% decarbonization by 2030 — but focused mainly on oil/energy

### The Ignored Emissions Sources

**1. Agriculture (the overlooked giant)**
The second largest GHG source receives far less policy attention than fossil fuels:

- Farmed livestock may account for **over 50% of all human GHG emissions** per year — far above the UN FAO's 12% estimate
- The discrepancy arises from methane's warming potential: **methane warms the planet 86x as much as CO₂** over the 10–20 years before decaying into CO₂
- Growing affluence in Asia and Latin America is driving global meat consumption higher, not lower

**2. Pets**
An almost entirely ignored emissions category:

- Global pet population: ~900 million dogs, ~600 million cats
- US dogs and cats alone: produce **64 million tons of CO₂ equivalent** per year (same impact as 13 million cars)
- Global pet food production: **106 million tons of CO₂ equivalent per year** (equivalent to all of Switzerland, Norway, and Denmark driving gasoline cars for a year)

**3. Digital Consumption**

- ICT (information and communications technology) accounts for **~2% of global GHG**
- AI, blockchain, IoT, and big data risk driving this significantly higher
- Every minute of streaming, social media, or video calling adds to the digital carbon footprint

**4. General Consumption**

- Americans: 4% of global population, **12% of global annual waste** (4.9 lbs/person/day)
- Clothing, household goods, personal products all carry embedded emissions
- Plant-based food market projected to reach **\$74.2B by 2027** — but scale relative to global meat consumption remains small

### The Political Constraint

> _"Politicians get very nervous when climate policies are presented in ways which would require or maybe even demand individual change."_ — James Dyke, Associate Professor in Earth Systems Science, University of Exeter

Net Zero requires changing what people eat, how they consume, and how they travel — not just swapping energy sources. This is politically undeliverable in most democracies, especially as developing nations are still raising living standards.

The fundamental equity tension: emerging economies that have only recently escaped poverty cannot reasonably be expected to forgo growth to correct emissions disproportionately caused by industrialised nations.

### ⚠️ IRA Stale Claim

Same stale-claim risk as the [[Carbon Capture and Sequestration]] paper. Both were written under Biden-era IRA assumptions. Under Trump (Jan 2025+), the IRA's clean energy provisions face repeal or erosion. The agricultural and consumption-side GHG arguments are policy-independent and remain valid; the IRA-dependent investment cases (CCS 45Q credits, renewable subsidies) should be verified.
