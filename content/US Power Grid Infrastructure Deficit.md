# US Power Grid Infrastructure Deficit

**Source:** Circuit Breaker | GQG Research (Sep 2024)
**Companion paper:** [[US Power Demand Surge]] (Charging Ahead, Sep 2024 — same date, demand side)
**Tags:** #power #grid #infrastructure #permitting #labor #transformers #bottleneck

---

## Summary

While [[US Power Demand Surge]] documents _why_ electricity demand is surging, Circuit Breaker documents _why supply cannot keep up_. GQG identified the US power grid itself — not generation capacity — as the single biggest bottleneck to the energy transition. Three interlocking constraints: a broken permitting regime, a severe labour shortage, and raw-material supply chain failures.

---

## The Scale of Required Investment

- Most of the existing US grid is near or past its useful life — requiring not just expansion but full replacement
- Investment needed: **5–10x historical annual levels**
- US annual transmission and distribution spend expected to rise from $78B to $98B by 2050
- \~70% of incremental spend through 2050 is at the **distribution** (local) level, not high-voltage transmission
- The US needs **47,300 GW-miles of new transmission by 2035** — a 57% increase — just to meet projected demand
- Average construction time for new facilities: **3 years**; major equipment lead times now **months long**

---

## Bottleneck 1: Permitting

- \~10,000 renewable energy projects representing nearly **2.6 Terawatts** of power are queued waiting for grid connection permission — double the number from two years prior
- Average time for a new power plant to receive approval: **5 years** (vs. ~2.5 years a decade ago)
- FERC is streamlining by ordering projects studied in bundles and prioritising those closest to construction — but an overlooked obstacle is **incumbent generator opposition**

> _"If you look carefully in some of the dockets of various public utility commissions around the country, you will find that generators will often officially become party to the case in order to oppose the transmission line… The reality is they don't want more supply to get to the market because they have that part of the market cornered."_
> — Former US public utility commission chairman

- Land access regulations need updating; solar requires 10–20x more land than gas; onshore wind up to **200x more land** per unit of electricity — making siting in populated areas almost impossible
- Multiple overlapping federal, state, and local permitting regimes add further delay

---

## Bottleneck 2: Labour Shortage

- Current US lineworkers: **120,000**
- Required by 2035: **~178,000** (50% increase)
- Current job openings: **21,000+**; demand growing 8.5% by 2026
- Training requirement: **5+ years** for skilled linework
- Generational challenge: younger workforce prefers gig-style employment; experienced lineworkers retiring faster than replacements can be trained

---

## Bottleneck 3: Transformer Supply Chain

- Over 90% of US electricity passes through large power transformers
- A multi-decade replacement programme is already underway as installed transformers pass their 35-year design life
- **Lead times for large transformers: 120 weeks** (average, 2024) — up sharply
- Root cause: shortage of **grain-oriented electrical steel (GOES)**, the critical input material
- GOES prices doubled since January 2020; copper up ~50%
- Even with easing, prices expected to remain volatile due to structural capacity constraints

---

## Investment Implication

GQG framed the grid constraint as an investment thesis in its own right — companies providing grid components (transformer manufacturers, cable producers, switchgear suppliers), engineering and construction services, and specialised grid materials stand to benefit from secular demand regardless of which energy sources ultimately win. The bottleneck is structural and multi-decade.

---

## ⚠️ Temporal Note — IRA Policy Risk

Like [[Carbon Capture and Sequestration]], Circuit Breaker was written (Sep 2024) under Biden-era IRA assumptions. The IRA's clean energy tax incentives drove the surge in projects in the interconnection queue. Under Trump (from Jan 2025), some of these incentives face headwinds — though the grid infrastructure deficit is structural and largely independent of any single policy.
