# Capital Cycle Theory — Software and Shale

**Source:** Is Software the New Shale? | GQG Research (Dec 2022)
**Tags:** #capital-cycle #software #SaaS #shale #valuation #GQG-philosophy #bear-case #2022

## Summary

December 2022 paper — GQG's early and accurate bear case on the cloud software/SaaS sector. Using the US shale oil boom-and-bust (2012–2016) as a structural analog, GQG argued that software had undergone the same capital-cycle distortion: easy capital flooded a real secular growth story, inflated unit economics metrics masked underlying unprofitability, and the eventual supply/demand normalisation would produce a multi-year downturn. The paper also articulates one of GQG's core investment principles: **the capital cycle applies to every sector without exception.**

---

## The Capital Cycle Thesis

### Core Adage

> _"Never underestimate capital seeking returns. Every sector, regardless of its current growth rate or theoretical pricing power, experiences a capital cycle at some point."_

The playbook is consistent across cycles:

1. A new, fast-growing technology convinces markets "this time is different"
2. New capital floods in
3. To mask deteriorating cash flows, companies and investors adopt novel metrics that appear to justify the high valuations
4. Supply eventually exceeds demand
5. A multi-year downturn follows — before the industry eventually matures into genuine profitability

---

## The Shale–Software Parallel

Both industries were considered "secular growth stories" within structurally modest industries (oil = 1–2% grower; IT spend = 4–5% grower). Both experienced the same distortion pattern:

| Dimension | US Shale (2012–2016) | Cloud Software/SaaS (2019–2022) |
|---|---|---|
| Growth narrative | Hydraulic fracturing — access to previously unreachable oil | Cloud architecture — easier deployment and adoption at scale |
| Bullish metrics | Oil well IRRs | Lifetime Value to Customer Acquisition Cost (LTV/CAC) |
| What metrics masked | Fixed costs, consolidated margins, capex | R\&D spend (~20% of revenue), SBC, consolidated losses |
| Capital markets reliance | Hundreds of billions in equity/debt financing | Hundreds of billions in VC and equity; SBC = hidden equity financing |
| Valuation at peak | Pioneer Natural Resources: 20–40x EPS in shale boom | Median software company: 6.1x revenue _after_ 61% drawdown |
| Post-peak trajectory | Multi-year production bust; eventual recovery on discipline | GQG forecast: multi-year revenue deceleration + margin compression |

### The SBC Problem

Share-based compensation in software was, in GQG's framing, **equivalent to issuing equity** and thus not "free" — yet it was excluded from almost all non-GAAP profitability metrics.

Zoom example: SBC rose to **27% of revenue** (vs. 10% in 2020), while GAAP operating margins collapsed. The cumulative external capital entering software **exceeded that of the then much-reviled shale industry.**

## The State of Software (as of Dec 2022)

Russell 1000 software companies founded post-2000:

- Median year-to-date drawdown: **61%** (through November 2022)
- Median price-to-revenue multiple: **6.1x** (still elevated despite the drawdown)
- GAAP profitability: only **22%** of the cohort
- Median GAAP LTM operating margin: **-19%**
- Median revenue growth deceleration from 2021 peak: **-9%** over 12 months

GQG's forward forecast (2023): further revenue deceleration, margin compression, and FCF decline — a "toxic mix" for any investment.

---

## What GQG Prescribed (For Companies)

Three steps to build sustainable software businesses:

1. Train customers to accept **≥2% annual price increases** — price to value, not to cost
2. Ruthlessly cut pet projects with no demonstrable ROI
3. Tie share-based compensation and variable pay to **business performance** (not guaranteed; reflects equity price reality)

GQG noted this requires a significant cultural shift that many software companies will resist.

## GQG's Own Track Record

GQG held select cloud software businesses before **cutting back in late 2021** when they first saw signs of deceleration and lack of margin improvement. This paper (Dec 2022) documents the retrospective validation of that exit decision. Consistent with the [[GQG Investment Philosophy]] principle of continuous reassessment as data changes.
