## Summary

GQG's Q2 2025 CIO letter, written after a period of underperformance relative to benchmark, articulates the mathematical case for why the S\&P 500's trailing returns are unsustainable and why GQG's defensive positioning — though costly in the short term — is the correct long-term posture. The paper introduces the term "variant perception" to describe GQG's deliberate divergence from consensus: at a moment of maximum narrative enthusiasm, they are anchoring to arithmetic. The letter draws on the Scott McNealy valuation framework, the TINA death thesis, and the P/S compression math to argue that high-quality compounders at moderate multiples are the only durable path to real returns. This paper is a direct predecessor to the full "coiled spring" formalisation in [[Defensive Sectors — 2026 Opportunity]].

## The S\&P 500 Return Decomposition

GQG breaks the S\&P 500's strong recent total returns into components — and finds the math uncomfortable:

- **Earnings CAGR:** ~8% (the genuine, sustainable component)
- **Multiple expansion:** Added another 12%+ per year in some periods — but this is a one-way ratchet that cannot continue indefinitely
- **Result:** Stock prices rose 20%+ annually while earnings grew at 8%

The implication: a significant portion of equity returns over the past several years has been borrowed from the future. Investors extrapolating recent total returns are extrapolating a component (multiple expansion) that is mathematically bounded — multiples cannot expand forever. Future returns must, by arithmetic, compress toward the earnings growth rate once multiples stabilise or mean-revert.

## TINA Is Dead

**TINA** ("There Is No Alternative" to equities) was the dominant post-GFC investing narrative, sustained by near-zero interest rates pushing investors into equities regardless of valuation. GQG declared this framework defunct:

- The US 10-year Treasury yield had been ~**4% for 33 consecutive months** as of mid-2025
- At 4% risk-free, equities must clear a far higher hurdle to justify their risk premium
- The earnings yield on the S\&P 500 (E/P) had compressed to levels where the risk-reward versus Treasuries was, in GQG's view, no longer compelling at the index level
- Capital that previously had "nowhere to go" now has a credible alternative: duration

This is a structural regime change, not a cyclical one. TINA sustained elevated multiples; its death removes one of the key supports for current valuations.

## The Price-to-Sales Math

GQG's most pointed quantitative argument concerns price-to-sales (P/S) multiples:

**The setup:**

- The **median P/S multiple for the top decile of S\&P 500 stocks** had reached ~10x
- Historically, when stocks trade at 10x revenues, the **median subsequent multiple compression is ~60%** — i.e., the market tends to de-rate these stocks by approximately 60% of the P/S premium over time

**The required growth to survive:**

- At 10x P/S with 60% median compression, an investor needs **~30% annual revenue growth** just to generate a 10% annualised total return after the multiple is re-rated
- Most of the stocks trading at 10x P/S cannot credibly sustain 30% revenue growth for the multi-year period required

**The Scott McNealy 2002 quote:**

> _"What were you thinking? At 10 times revenues, to give you back your money in 10 years, I have to pay you 100% of revenues for 10 straight years in dividends. That assumes I can get that by my shareholders. That assumes I have zero cost of goods sold. That assumes zero expenses. That assumes I pay no taxes on my revenues... Do you realize how ridiculous those basic assumptions are?"_

McNealy (Sun Microsystems CEO) said this at the post-bubble trough — when 10x P/S stocks had already crashed. GQG is applying the same logic _before_ the crash, which is the point of variant perception.

## Tech CapEx at Dotcom Highs

A structural data point used to triangulate bubble magnitude:

- Technology capital expenditure as a **percentage of GDP** had reached levels last seen at the peak of the dotcom bubble (1999–2000)
- This is not merely a valuation signal — it reflects actual resource misallocation: physical data centres, chips, and power infrastructure being built to serve a demand curve that may not materialise
- GQG's capital cycle framework ([[Capital Cycle Theory — Software and Shale]]) predicts that excess capital deployment triggers competitive oversupply, margin compression, and eventual write-downs — regardless of how transformative the underlying technology is

---

## Index Concentration Risk

- The top 10 S\&P 500 companies represented **~35% of total index market cap** as of mid-2025 — historically anomalous concentration
- This creates hidden beta in "diversified" index portfolios: investors who think they own 500 companies effectively own a concentrated bet on 10
- A de-rating of the top decile would disproportionately impact total return for passive investors who have no defensives exposure

## GQG's Positioning Response

Despite underperformance in Q2 2025, GQG held firm and continued rotating toward:

- **High single-digit to low double-digit EPS compounders** at moderate multiples (mid-teens or lower)
- **3–4% dividend yields** providing a return floor independent of multiple expansion
- **Businesses with genuine demand visibility** — utilities (regulated earnings), staples (inelastic demand), insurance (actuarial compounding)
- **Avoidance of narrative-dependent stories** — any stock requiring sustained 30%+ revenue growth to justify its current P/S multiple

> _"Variant perception, by definition, is uncomfortable. We are not predicting a crash. We are declining to pay 10x revenues for growth rates that cannot sustain 10x revenues."_
