## Summary

GQG's five-part research series (Sep 2025 – Apr 2026) is their most comprehensive and rigorous case for why the current AI-driven tech market is not merely overvalued but structurally worse than the dotcom bubble. The series moved from the macro valuation argument (Part I) to a specific company dissection of OpenAI (Part II) to the accounting mechanics of how hyperscaler CapEx is inflating S\&P 500 earnings (Part III). ## Part I: The Macro Case (Sep 2025)

### "Forward-Looking Quality" vs "Backward-Looking Quality"

GQG's core investment philosophy centres on owning "Forward-Looking Quality" — businesses where the best days lie ahead. Part I marked the **first time in GQG's history** they declared major technology companies represent **"Backward-Looking Quality"** — quality that was real in the past but is no longer forward-looking.

Evidence: revenue growth has structurally decelerated. Microsoft and Nvidia already control ~60% of software and semiconductor profits respectively — sustaining supernormal growth from a dominant base is "virtually impossible." Long-term revenue growth projected to single digits within five years.

### Why This May Be Worse Than Dotcom

Three myths the market believes vs. GQG's rebuttal:

| Myth | GQG's View |
|---|---|
| Tech companies today are higher quality than in 2000 | False — revenue growth decelerating; FCF margins deteriorating; CapEx as % of sales hitting new highs |
| Tech companies today are cheaper than in 2000 | False — on forward quality-adjusted basis, current valuations are comparable or worse |
| The broader market is cheaper than in 2000 | False — today's market, outside tech, is also expensive vs. history |

Additional reason this could be _worse_: the scale of the AI bet relative to the economy and market is far greater than 1999-2000.

Sam Altman (quoted by GQG): _"When bubbles happen, smart people get overexcited about a kernel of truth. Are we in a phase where investors as a whole are overexcited about AI? My opinion is yes."_

### Dotcom Parallels (Part I)

**Narrative parallel 1 — US Exceptionalism:**
In 1999, US GDP was growing 5%, emerging markets had collapsed (Asian crisis, Russian default), and the "American Age of Affluence" drove TINA capital flows. Today, the same narrative underpins the AI rally — US tech dominance, "TINA" trade. A Paine Webber research note from November 1999 predicting Cisco's continued dominance reads word-for-word like AI bullishness today.

**Narrative parallel 2 — Revolutionary Technology:**
The internet was real and did change the world. AI is real and probably will too. The mistake in both cases was conflating a real technology transformation with the investability of the companies riding the initial CapEx wave at any price.

IBM near the dotcom peak: _"The \[internet] revolution has arrived. With stunning speed, it has swept all of us into a new kind of economy..."_ GQG: "We believe this sounds like AI today."

**Track record:** GQG was "comparatively larger buyers of Nvidia in 2023." They saw the revolution early. They also knew when to exit.

## Part III: The Accounting Illusion (Dec 2025)

See dedicated page: [[Hyperscaler CapEx Circular Accounting]]

Key finding: GQG estimates ~$540B in AI-related spending (hyperscalers + VC) in 2025 contributed **~34% of the S&P 500's ~$586B operating income growth\*\* through a cascading revenue recognition effect. When CapEx growth slows, the reversal will be severe. Semiconductors now ~15% of S\&P 500 (vs. ~4% historical median) — unprecedented index cyclicality.

## Part IV: The Debt Iceberg (Mar 2026)

See dedicated page: [[AI Data Center Debt Bubble]]

Key findings: Data center debt issuance surged to $183B in 2025 (from $92B); Morgan Stanley projects a $1.5T financing gap through 2028. The real fragility is in **neoclouds** — smaller builders responsible for 64% of forthcoming data center supply, now holding $32B+ in debt with little credit history. **CoreWeave** is the poster child: $21.4B in debt at 10%+ rates against $5.1B in 2025 revenue, nearly all consumed by debt service. GPU-collateralized loans at 15% interest (DDTL 1.0 from Blackstone/Magnetar). GQG's thesis: when the cycle turns, fractures will appear first in smaller developers, not hyperscalers.

---

## Part V: Too Big to Permit (Apr 2026)

See dedicated page: [[Data Center Social License Risk]]

Key findings: 78 proposed data centers faced major roadblocks between Jan 2025 and Feb 2026; 40%+ were withdrawn. Bipartisan opposition (65%+ in Republican-leaning states). Data centers' share of US electricity is heading from 6% to 12% by 2028, driving PJM capacity auction prices from $30/MW-day to $329/MW-day. Diesel costs surged 33% since late February 2026 (Persian Gulf conflict). GQG frames the loss of "social license to operate" as an underappreciated crack in the bubble.

## The Through-Line Across All Five Parts

The series builds a coherent, layered argument:

1. The narrative is the same as dotcom (US exceptionalism + revolutionary tech) but the scale is larger
2. The underlying businesses (especially those funding the AI build-out) have deteriorating fundamentals
3. The accounting is inflating reported profits, masking the true trajectory
4. One key company (OpenAI) is the linchpin holding the entire ecosystem's valuations together via circular financing
5. The financing structure itself is fragile — concentrated in highly leveraged neoclouds facing binary outcomes
6. Physical and political constraints (permits, power, water, community opposition) make monetisation timelines slower and more expensive than consensus assumes
7. When the cycle turns — whether from CapEx deceleration, AGI doubt, external geopolitical shock, or debt covenant stress — the reversal will be fast and severe
